Motivation and relevance 1

  • In 2019, almost 60% of children under age 6 attended one or more weekly nonparental care arrangements (USDOE, 2021)
    • States have adopted regulations that set minimum standards for child care facilities and programs
    • Structural features of care settings thought to be related to quality and safety, such as staff-child ratios (SCR)
  • SCR strictness impacts childcare establishments through opposing forces
    • #1: ↑ employment, ↑ service quality (Garcia-Vazquez, 2023)
    • #2: ↑ service costs → ↓ supply curve, ↓ demand for ECE employment
    • #2: ↑ service costs → ↓ Affordability of ECE → ↑ alternative care
    • The combined effect of these two opposing forces requires empirical investigation
[Click] To talk about motivation for this study, Nonparental care arragement is very common and there is a need for providing high quality of care. States set minimum standards for child care facilities, and most of them are structural that are thought to be related to quality and safety. [Click]

We focus on staff to child ratios becasuse impact of staff/child ratio is ambiguous as they tend to have two potential opposesing forces on the market. Setting higher staff to child ratio requires more staff per child, which means it leads to more employment; and more staff per child means higher service quality. But, increasing staff raises service cost and it is passed onto consumers, and as it becomes less affordable, consumers will look for other althernatives and demand for care would fall along with the employment. The overall impact of these two opposing forces needs to be studied empirically.

Motivation and relevance 2

  • Relatively few empirical study
    • Hotz and Xiao (2011)
      • Establishment-level panel data on child care centers from 1987 to 1997, finds:
        • stricter state regulation of staff-child ratios led to a decrease in the number of child care center establishments particularly in poor communities.
        • improved quality of care proxied by the share of child care centers with national accreditation, particularly in higher income areas.
and There is relatively few studies that have examined the effects of ratio on the child care market. There is a Hotz and Xiao paper published in 2011, but it uses data from 25 years ago, so we are re-examining using the newer data, and different methods. -------------------------------- who use restricted-access establishment-level panel data on child care centers from 1987, 1992 and 1997 in a model with state, time and establishment fixed effects. Their results show that stricter state regulation of staff-child ratios led to a decrease in the number of child care center establishments, particularly in areas with more poor families. Yet they also found evidence that stricter state regulation improved quality of care proxied by the share of child care centers with national accreditation, particularly in higher income areas

This study

  • Re-examine Hotz and Xiao (2011)’s results using more recent data and three different quasi-experimental designs
    • Border regression discontinuity based on small geographic unit data within 10 miles of the border line
      • U.S. childcare provider’s exact location from Dun & Bradstreet (D&B) and estimates SCR effects focusing on fineresolution differences across state borders
      • The effect of stricter regulations on the number of establishments, their payroll and number of employees
    • Two-way fixed effects design based on the county-level data
      • 2011, 2014, 2017 County Business Patterns data on childcare industry establishments, employment, and payroll
    • Synthetic control based on state-level data
      • Aggregated from 2011 to 2021 data from County Business Patterns data on childcare industry establishments, employment, and payroll
      • Exact date of the policy change is known and uses a synthetic control group approach
so, based on newer data from 2011 to 2017, and we are implementing three different quasi-experimental designs. First, we use regression discontinuity design based on small geographic unit data within 10 miles of the border line. We have childcare provider’s exact location from Dun & Bradstreet, and estimate how market outcome changes as it goes from lower/stricter policy states. \\ Second, We use county-level data and examine the effect of changing ratio requirement using two-way fixed effects model with county and year fixed effects, which is close to the study by Hotz and Xiao. \\ Third, we use state-year level data to implement synthetic control method. so for each treated state, we identified the specific year when the policy change occurred, and construct a “synthetic state” to compare if market outcomes have different trajectory between the treated and control state. --------------------- One concern with these two models is that there could be local economic shocks correlated with state changes in SCR. A different design focuses on contrasts across state borders.

Variation in the policy variable (Staff to child ratio)

This graph shows the variation in staff to child ratio over time during the study window, there is a couple of states that experienced a decrease in the ratio ranging from 5% to 9% between 2011 to 14, few states saw an increase in the ratio ranging 4% to 12% between 2014 and 2017. --------------------------------- The ratio information comes from Child Care Licensing Programs and Policies Survey. They publish data on ratios for each state by child age group every three years, and we use data from 2011 to 2017. Across the 6,504 county-years from 2,168 unique counties in the county-level analysis, this raw SCR variable averages 0.12 (average regulation requires 0.12 staff per child or 8.33 children per staff) with a standard deviation of 0.026, a minimum of 0.0 (in state-years with no SCR regulation) and a maximum of 0.167 (6 children per staff) in the most-stringent regime. If the minimum is 1 staff per 4 children, the staff-child minimum ratio is 0.25. If there is no minimum, the value is 0, corresponding to a possibly infinite number of children per staff. To build a harmonized summary measure of SCR stringency for each state year that includes information across age groups, we take a weighted average of group SCR using each age group’s share of the 66 months of age before average age at kindergarten entry. Across all states in all three calendar years, SCR policies are observed for 5 age points. We assume each age-point policy applies for the following share of early childhood: 6-week SCR gets 12/66 months of weight, 18-month gets 24/66, 3-year gets 12/66, 4-year gets 12/66, and 5-year gets 6/66 months. For each state-year, we use the cross-age-point weighted average of these SCR to a scalar measure SCR policy overall.

Summary statistics for county-level analysis

The balanced panel analytic dataset includes 2,168 counties and it is a 3 year panel, (that has more than one providers in each of the three years from 2011 to 2017). The outcome variables consist of payroll per employee, employment counts, and the number of establishments by size from County Business Patterns (CBP) data, along with turnover rates sourced from the Census Quarterly Workforce Indicators data. Staff-to-child ratio is a state-level variable, the mean across the county-year is 0.115, which is 8.9 children per staff. Demographic controls are based on county-level ACS 5-year estimates for the 2011, 2014, and 2017 snapshots.

Summary statistics for regression discontinuity design

For zone-level data, The unit of analysis is the zone-state-subzone combination, which divides zones based on state borders and distance to border, we have three year panel and 5775 observations. The outcome variables are the number of establishments, employees, and Sales volume sourced from DNB data, we normalize them by number of families with young children. Demographic controls at the zone level are derived from Block-group level ACS data. ------------------ We have 311 zones across the county and we divided them into 2-mile block to the border line lead to 2268 zone-state-miles to border units.

County-year level analysis: Effect of child-staff ratio on number of establishment per family by employee size

Moving on to the resutls, The county panel analysis shows no overall effect of changes in SCR regulation on the number of childcare establishments per family with any young children. The point estimates are negative but not significant without controls with and without controls. when looking at the establishment by size of employees, establishments with larger size with 20 to 39 employees show negative and statistically significant effect. Traslating the coefficent in coulmn (11) says that one SD increase in the ratio induces 5% reduction in the total establishments , which is consistent with findings by Hotz and Xiao (2011) in that greater stringency of regulations reduces number of center-based providers. ---------------- Increasing required staff per child by 1 SD(like moving from 13 children allowed per staff to 10 allowed), induces 0.016(=0.026*0.645) reduction (3.1% of the outcome mean). But the estimate is not significant at conventional levels. The point estimates here agree with Hotz and Xiao (2011) but the estimate is imprecise. Looking at effects across establishment employment-size categories, none of the models including controls are statistically significant, except for establishments with larger size with 20 to 39 employees, where one SD increase in the ratio induces -0.026(=-1.126*0.026) reduction in the establishments which is approximately 5% of the total number of establishment per 100 families, which is consistent with findings by Hotz and Xiao (2011) in that greater stringency of regulations reduces number of center-based providers.

County-year level analysis: Effect of child-staff ratio on N of employees, N of establishments, annual payroll per Employee, and staff turnover rate

The change in ratio doesn’t seem to have affect other outcome variables including number of total childcare employees, payroll per employee and staff turnover rate. The signs are negative but not statistically significant at the conventional level. --------------------------------- A fall in payroll could be a compensating differential for an improved working condition of higher SCR. It could also just be a squeeze on workers by businesses facing tighter margins as regulations force increases in staffing quantity per child. We do not see strong evidence of effects on the number of employees per family with any young child (staffing intensity) nor staff turnover rate (proxy for low job quality). Alternatively, providers may be cutting hourly wages and substituting towards workers with worse outside options.

Conclusion

  • County-level Analysis
    • Reveals no statistically significant impacts of SCR regulation changes on the number of establishments, payroll, or turnover
    • Stricter regulations are associated with modest reductions in medium-sized providers (20–39 employees), particularly in low-income areas.
    • ↑ SCR → ↑ service costs → ↓ supply curve, ↓ demand for ECE employment
  • Zone-level Analysis
    • In zones within 10 miles of state borders, stricter regulations correlate with a significant increase in childcare employment in lower-income areas.
    • ↑ SCR → ↑ employment, ↑ service quality (Garcia-Vazquez, 2023)
  • State-level Analysis
    • Prelimiary results, but Louisiana results seem to indicate no clear impact of adoptation of policy change on the child care market outcomes.
so to summarize what we found so far,

we find stricter child-to-staff ratio (SCR) regulations have no statistically significant impacts on market outcomes, but we find some evidence that modest reductions in medium-sized providers (20–39 employees), particularly in low-income areas. This seems to align with the one of the forces mentioned earlier, that stricter policy increases cost of service and leads to decline in the supply and demand for the service.

Zone-level analysis finds that stricter regulation in areas within 10 miles of state borders increase childcare employment in lower-income areas. This seems to contradicting the finding from County-level analysis. so, we are trying to make sense out of the results.

Preliminary state-level results for Louisiana, show no clear impact of the policy change on child care market outcomes.

So, Overall, we find very little evidence of the impact of staff-to-child ratios on market outcomes. We are working on refining the policy variables by identifying the exact year of these policy changes. and This will allow us to include more years in the data and increase the power of our estimation.
