Motivation and relevance 1

  • In 2019, almost 60% of children under age 6 attended one or more weekly nonparental care setting (USDOE, 2021)
    • States have adopted regulations that set minimum standards for child care facilities and programs
    • Structural features of care settings thought to be related to quality and safety
  • Policy relevance
    • Understanding the role of regulation on the market is important as it might correlate with supply and price of care
    • Increasing interest in expansion of child care by deregulation
  • SCR strictness impacts childcare establishments through opposing forces
    • #1: ↑ employment, ↑ service quality → ↑ demand for ECE ↑ Q
    • #2: ↑ service costs → ↓ supply curve, ↓ demand for ECE employment ↓ Q
    • #2a: ↑ service costs → ↓ affordability of ECE → ↑ alternative care ↓ Q
    • The combined effect of these two opposing forces requires empirical investigation

Why is regulation important? Understanding the role of regulation in the market is crucial, as it may influence the supply and cost of care. There is growing interest in expanding child care through deregulation, and it is important to understand how regulation impacts the market to prevent any unintended negative effects from both regulation and deregulation.

In this study, we focus on staff to child ratios becasuse its impact is ambiguous as they tend to have two potential opposesing forces on the market. First, and more intuitive force is that Setting higher staff to child ratio requires more staff per child, which means it leads to more employment; and more staff per child means higher service quality, which leads to higher demand for the care. But, increasing staff raises service cost and it's passed onto consumers, and as it becomes less affordable, consumers will look for other althernatives and demand for child care would fall along with the overall quantity in the market. so, the net impact of these two opposing forces needs to be studied empirically.

Motivation and relevance 2

  • Relatively few empirical studies
    • Hotz and Xiao (2011)
      • Establishment-level panel data on child care centers from 1987 to 1997, finds:
        • stricter state regulation of staff-child ratios led to a decrease in the number of child care center establishments particularly in poor communities.
        • improved quality of care proxied by the share of child care centers with national accreditation, particularly in higher income areas.
and There is relatively few studies that have examined the effects of the ratio on the child care market. There is a Hotz and Xiao paper published in 2011, and they found stricter staff to child ratios decrease number of child care center establishments, particularly in poor communities. but it uses data from 25 years ago, so we are re-examining using the newer data, and different methods.

-------------------------------- who use restricted-access establishment-level panel data on child care centers from 1987, 1992 and 1997 in a model with state, time and establishment fixed effects. Their results show that stricter state regulation of staff-child ratios led to a decrease in the number of child care center establishments, particularly in areas with more poor families. Yet they also found evidence that stricter state regulation improved quality of care proxied by the share of child care centers with national accreditation, particularly in higher income areas

This study

  • Re-examine Hotz and Xiao (2011)’s results using more recent data and three different quasi-experimental designs
    • 1. Two-way fixed effects design based on the county-level data
      • 2011, 2014, 2017 County Business Patterns data on childcare industry establishments, employment, and payroll
    • 2. Border regression discontinuity based on small geographic unit data within 10 miles of the border line
      • U.S. childcare provider’s exact location from Dun & Bradstreet (D&B) and estimates SCR effects focusing on fineresolution differences across state borders
      • The effect of stricter regulations on the number of establishments, their payroll and number of employees
    • 3. Synthetic control based on state-level data
      • Aggregated from 2011 to 2021 data from County Business Patterns data on childcare industry establishments, employment, and payroll
      • Exact date of the policy change is known and uses a synthetic control group approach
so, Using newer data from 2011 to 2017, we are implementing three different quasi-experimental designs.

First , We use county business pattern data and estimation is based on two-way fixed effects model with county and year fixed effects, which is close to the study by Hotz and Xiao in terms of method.

Second, we use regression discontinuity design based on small geographic unit data within 10 miles of the border line. We have childcare provider’s exact location from Dun & Bradstreet data, and estimate how market outcome changes as it goes from lower/stricter policy states.

Third, we use state-year level data to implement synthetic control method. so for each treated state, we identified the specific year when the policy change occurred, and construct a “synthetic state” to compare if market outcomes have different trajectory between the treated and the counterfactual.

--------------------- One concern with these two models is that there could be local economic shocks correlated with state changes in SCR. A different design focuses on contrasts across state borders.

County-year level analysis: Effect of child-staff ratio on N of employees, N of establishments, annual payroll per Employee, and staff turnover rate

Moving on to the results, Based on the two-way fixed effects model, the change in ratio doesn’t seem to have affect any observed outcomes. The signs are negative but not statistically significant at the conventional level.

--------------------------------- A fall in payroll could be a compensating differential for an improved working condition of higher SCR. It could also just be a squeeze on workers by businesses facing tighter margins as regulations force increases in staffing quantity per child. We do not see strong evidence of effects on the number of employees per family with any young child (staffing intensity) nor staff turnover rate (proxy for low job quality). Alternatively, providers may be cutting hourly wages and substituting towards workers with worse outside options. ---------------- Increasing required staff per child by 1 SD(like moving from 13 children allowed per staff to 10 allowed), induces 0.016(=0.026*0.645) reduction (3.1% of the outcome mean). But the estimate is not significant at conventional levels. The point estimates here agree with Hotz and Xiao (2011) but the estimate is imprecise. Looking at effects across establishment employment-size categories, none of the models including controls are statistically significant, except for establishments with larger size with 20 to 39 employees, where one SD increase in the ratio induces -0.026(=-1.126*0.026) reduction in the establishments which is approximately 5% of the total number of establishment per 100 families, which is consistent with findings by Hotz and Xiao (2011) in that greater stringency of regulations reduces number of center-based providers.

So to summarize what we found so far, this table summarizes our findings and Hotz and Xias findings as a reference.

We find stricter child-to-staff ratio (SCR) regulations have no impacts on overall market outcomes using data from 2011-17, although we find some evidence that modest reductions in the number of providers in low-income areas. The signs of the estimates mostly align with what was found in Hotz and Xiao, and seems to align with one of the forces mentioned earlier, that stricter policy increases cost of service and leads to a decline in the supply and demand for the service.

In RDD analysis, we find that stricter regulation in areas within 10 miles of state borders increase childcare employment in lower-income areas, which seem to align with the 1st force that more stringent regulation increase number of staffs per child.

So, Overall, we find limited evidence of the impact of staff-to-child ratios on market outcomes. We are working on refining the policy variables by identifying the exact year of these policy changes. Appendix: Variation in the policy variable (Staff to child ratio)

Here you can see the variation in staff to child ratio over time during the study window, there is a couple of states that experienced a decrease in the ratio between 2011 to 14, and few states saw an increase in the ratio up to 13 percent between 2014 and 17. But most states have no change in the ratio during the study window.

--------------------------------- The ratio information comes from Child Care Licensing Programs and Policies Survey. They publish data on ratios for each state by child age group every three years, and we use data from 2011 to 2017. Across the 6,504 county-years from 2,168 unique counties in the county-level analysis, this raw SCR variable averages 0.12 (average regulation requires 0.12 staff per child or 8.33 children per staff) with a standard deviation of 0.026, a minimum of 0.0 (in state-years with no SCR regulation) and a maximum of 0.167 (6 children per staff) in the most-stringent regime. If the minimum is 1 staff per 4 children, the staff-child minimum ratio is 0.25. If there is no minimum, the value is 0, corresponding to a possibly infinite number of children per staff. To build a harmonized summary measure of SCR stringency for each state year that includes information across age groups, we take a weighted average of group SCR using each age group’s share of the 66 months of age before average age at kindergarten entry. Across all states in all three calendar years, SCR policies are observed for 5 age points. We assume each age-point policy applies for the following share of early childhood: 6-week SCR gets 12/66 months of weight, 18-month gets 24/66, 3-year gets 12/66, 4-year gets 12/66, and 5-year gets 6/66 months. For each state-year, we use the cross-age-point weighted average of these SCR to a scalar measure SCR policy overall.

This graph shows how the distribution of providers differs near state borders with varying level of staff-child ratio.

The x-axis is the distance from the border, with negative values in the less-strict state and positive values in the stricter state. The vertical axis is the share of providers at each distance.

so the figure on top assumes no differences in regulation, and so providers are evenly distributed across the border. Figure at the bottom assumes there exists difference in the regulation between states, and if stricter regulation increase costs more than demand, supply shifts toward the less-strict side, as providers move to maximize profits.

-------- This graph shows how the spatial distribution of provider establishments or employment around a state border might differ under two scenarios where a pair of bordering states have different CSR strictness in policy. The horizontal axis is the distance to the border with negative numbers indicating distance into the less-strict state and positive ones into the more-strict state. The vertical axis is the share of local childcare establishments located at each distance from the border. If there is no regulatory differences, we would expect to see childcare providers uniformly distributed across the border, just like in figure (a) If SCR regulation drives down supply through increased costs more than it drives up demand through increased quality, then we would expect to see a pattern as in sub-figure (b), where some of the supply that would otherwise locate near the strict side of the border can boost profit by crossing the border.
